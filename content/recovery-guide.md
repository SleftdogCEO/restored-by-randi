# Post-Mastectomy Recovery Guide

*A compassionate, week-by-week guide to help you heal — body and spirit.*

---

You've just been through one of the most challenging experiences of your life. Whether you had a single mastectomy, double mastectomy, or lumpectomy, the road ahead is uniquely yours. This guide is here to walk beside you — not to replace your medical team, but to give you a warm, honest picture of what to expect.

**You are brave. You are strong. And you are not alone.**

---

## Weeks 1–2: The Immediate Recovery

### What to Expect Physically
- **Soreness and tightness** in your chest, armpit, and sometimes down your arm
- **Surgical drains** — usually 1-2, which your surgeon will remove when output decreases (typically 1-2 weeks)
- **Limited arm mobility** — you won't be able to lift your arms above your head yet, and that's okay
- **Fatigue** — your body is healing from major surgery. Rest is not laziness; rest is medicine
- **Numbness or tingling** around the incision site — this is normal and may persist for months

### What You Can Do
- **Wear a soft, front-closing surgical bra** or camisole for support and comfort
- **Use pillows** to prop yourself up while sleeping — sleeping slightly elevated helps with swelling
- **Take short, gentle walks** around the house to promote circulation
- **Accept help.** Let people bring meals, drive you to appointments, do your laundry. This is not the time for independence.
- **Keep a small notebook** to track drain output, medications, and questions for your doctor

### Emotional Check-in
It's completely normal to feel grief, anger, relief, numbness, or all of these at once. Your body has changed, and it takes time to process that. If you feel like crying, cry. If you feel like laughing at something ridiculous on TV, laugh. There's no right way to feel.

---

## Weeks 3–4: Gradual Improvement

### What to Expect Physically
- **Drains are usually out** by now — what a relief!
- **Swelling starts to decrease** but will continue changing for several months
- **Incisions are healing** — follow your surgeon's wound care instructions carefully
- **You may begin gentle stretching** as cleared by your surgeon
- **Energy starts returning** in small waves

### What You Can Do
- **Begin arm exercises** as recommended by your medical team — gentle stretches to restore range of motion
- **Start considering a lightweight temporary breast form** (a "puff" or fiber-fill form) for under clothing
- **Resume light daily activities** — cooking simple meals, short errands
- **Continue walking** — gradually increase distance

### Emotional Check-in
This is often when the "busyness" of surgery fades and emotions hit harder. You might look in the mirror and feel shock or sadness. That's a natural part of grieving. Consider connecting with a support group — talking to women who understand can be incredibly healing.

---

## Weeks 5–6: Finding Your New Normal

### What to Expect Physically
- **Significant improvement** in range of motion
- **Incisions continue to mature** — they'll be red/pink for a while, then gradually fade
- **You may be cleared to drive** (usually once you can turn and look over your shoulder comfortably)
- **Sensitivity changes** — some areas may remain numb, others may become hypersensitive

### What You Can Do
- **Try a lightweight temporary form** in a soft pocketed bra — this can make getting dressed feel more "normal"
- **Begin physical therapy** if prescribed — especially important if you had lymph node removal
- **Gradually resume more activities** — light housework, social outings
- **Start thinking about your permanent fitting** — most women are ready at 6-8 weeks

### Physical Therapy Focus
If you had lymph nodes removed, physical therapy is crucial for:
- **Preventing lymphedema** — chronic swelling in the arm
- **Restoring shoulder mobility**
- **Rebuilding strength gradually**
- **Learning self-massage and drainage techniques**

Ask your surgeon for a referral to a therapist experienced with post-mastectomy patients.

## Weeks 8–12: Settling In

### What to Expect
- **Your permanent prosthesis becomes part of your daily routine**
- **Continued healing** — scars fade, range of motion improves
- **You may be starting or continuing** radiation, chemotherapy, or hormone therapy
- **Energy levels fluctuate** — be patient with yourself

### What You Can Do
- **Explore different bra styles** — everyday, sports, lace, strapless options
- **Consider swimwear** if you enjoy water activities — mastectomy swimsuits are beautiful and functional
- **Keep up with physical therapy exercises** even after formal PT ends
- **Start or continue gentle exercise** — walking, yoga, swimming (once cleared)

---

## Long-Term Recovery & Wellness

### Physical Wellness
- **Annual fittings** — your body continues to change; an annual check ensures your fit stays perfect
- **Replace your prosthesis** every 1-2 years (covered by insurance)
- **Up to 6 mastectomy bras per year** are covered by most insurance plans
- **Watch for lymphedema signs** — persistent swelling, heaviness, or tightness in your arm
- **Stay active** — regular exercise improves energy, mood, and long-term health

### Emotional Wellness
- **There is no timeline for emotional recovery.** Give yourself grace.
- **Consider counseling** — many cancer centers offer free or low-cost therapy
- **Join a support group** — in-person or online, connecting with other survivors is powerful
- **Celebrate milestones** — your surgery anniversary, finishing treatment, your first swim
- **Be honest with loved ones** about what you need — they want to help but may not know how

### When to Call Your Doctor
- Signs of infection (redness, warmth, unusual discharge from incisions)
- Persistent or worsening arm swelling
- Emotional distress that interferes with daily life
- Any new lumps or changes
